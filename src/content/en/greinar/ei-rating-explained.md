The numbers in a fire-resistance class like EI 60 look obvious — sixty minutes. The letters in front of them are less obvious, and they are the part that decides whether the rating actually applies to your situation.

## What E means

**E** is integrity. It means the barrier does not let flame or hot gases pass through to the unexposed side. Cracks, displaced products and joint failure all break E.

## What I means

**I** is insulation. It means the unexposed side of the barrier does not heat up beyond a defined limit (180 K average rise, 140 K at any single point). Even with E intact, a glowing-hot barrier can ignite combustible material on the other side. That is why I matters.

## The minute numbers

The number after EI is the time, in minutes, that the barrier maintains both E and I in the standard fire test. EI 60 means sixty minutes; EI 120 means two hours; EI 240 means four hours.

## When you need each

- **EI 60** is the typical minimum for compartmentation between rooms in commercial buildings.
- **EI 120** is required for separation between buildings, between major fire compartments and around critical infrastructure (transformer rooms, escape stair cores).
- **EI 240** is for the highest-risk separations — between buildings of different occupancy, large fuel stores, and similar.
