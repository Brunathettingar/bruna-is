We do a lot of inspections of older buildings, and the same problems show up again and again. Some are cosmetic. Some are full breaches of fire compartmentation. Here are seven of the most common.

## 1. Wrong product, right place

A penetration is sealed — but the product used is not certified for that configuration. Foam where caulk was tested. Sealant on a cable bundle the manufacturer never tested at that fill rate.

## 2. Right product, wrong installation

The right product is in place, but the depth, the backing or the surrounding gap doesn't match the tested system. The ETA was for a 100 mm wall; the actual wall is 75 mm.

## 3. Penetrations added after handover

A new cable is pulled through an existing penetration. Nobody re-seals to the original system. The compartmentation rating is broken from that day on.

## 4. Joints treated as walls

A movement joint or wall-to-slab joint is sealed with wall-grade product. The joint then opens with normal building movement and the seal cracks.

## 5. No installer record

The work was done correctly, but there is no record of who did it, when, or what product. At inspection there is nothing to point to.

## 6. Generic mortar

Plain mortar or grout is used to fill openings instead of certified fire-rated mortar. They look the same. They do not perform the same.

## 7. Missed openings

Holes drilled late in the build, or temporary openings for site logistics, are simply never sealed at all.
