The Icelandic building code — Byggingarreglugerð 112/2012 — sets the fire-resistance requirements for compartmentation in commercial buildings. The headline ratings like EI 60 and EI 120 are easy to look up. The harder questions are what has to happen at every cable, pipe and duct that crosses a rated wall or slab, and how you prove at handover that the work was done to a tested system.

## What the code requires

Chapter 9 of the code covers fire compartmentation: minimum ratings by occupancy, protected escape routes, and the rules for penetrations. The principle is straightforward — a fire-rated barrier has to maintain its rating across its full area, and every hole cut through it has to be re-rated to match.

## Where projects typically run into trouble

Three problems recur on commercial work:

1. **Uncertified sealing.** A wall is EI 90 on paper, but penetrations are filled with generic mortar or untested expanding foam. The wall is no longer compliant.
2. **Missing documentation.** Even when the work is correct, the project can't produce manufacturer datasheets, ETAs or installer competence records at handover.
3. **Fire sealing brought in too late.** Scope is finalised after MEP routes are fixed, forcing field compromises that are then hard to document after the fact.

## What the fire authority looks for

Building inspectors want to see that every penetration matches a tested ETA or classified system, that the installer is competent, and that the as-built record traces back through the drawings to the products on the wall.
