Structural steel loses its load-bearing capacity quickly in a fire. Intumescent paint is the most common way to protect it — a thin coating that swells into a thick char layer when heated, slowing the temperature rise of the steel underneath. The European standard that governs it is EN 13381-8.

## What EN 13381-8 covers

The standard sets the test procedure for measuring the contribution of reactive coatings to the fire resistance of steel members. The output is a set of tables — for each section factor (A/V) and each fire-resistance time, what coating thickness gives that protection.

## How to read manufacturer data

Manufacturer datasheets reference EN 13381-8 and present thickness tables by section factor. To use them correctly you need:

- the section factor of the actual steel member (not a generic value)
- the required fire-resistance class — R 30, R 60, R 90, R 120 or R 240
- the critical temperature of the steel (typically 500 °C, sometimes higher for non-load-bearing applications)

A common error is reading the table for the wrong section factor. A heavier section needs less paint; a lighter one needs more. Get this wrong and the coating is either expensive overkill or, more dangerously, under-rated.

## R 30 to R 240

The R numbers — R 30 through R 240 — use the same minute system as EI, but R is for load-bearing capacity. The required R class comes from the building code based on the building's height, use and load.
