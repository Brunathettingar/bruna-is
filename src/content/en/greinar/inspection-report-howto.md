An inspection report on an older building tells the fire authority — and the owner — what state the building's fire compartmentation is in today. It is the starting point for any remediation work and a defensive document when the building changes hands.

## When you need one

You need an inspection report when:

- the fire authority requests one during an inspection cycle
- the building is being sold or refinanced
- substantial renovation is planned and fire compartmentation could be affected
- the building has had unmanaged change over many years and no current register exists

## What it contains

A useful inspection report walks the building, opening by opening, and records:

- the location and type of each penetration, joint or seal
- the current condition, with photographic evidence
- whether the existing sealing matches a known tested system
- a recommendation for each non-compliant item — re-seal, replace, or leave with note

## How it helps at fire authority inspection

The fire authority does not want to find problems for the owner. They want a clear current statement of where the building stands, and a credible remediation plan for anything outstanding. An inspection report gives both — and turns an inspection visit into a structured conversation rather than a surprise.
